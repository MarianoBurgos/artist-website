About This Project

This website presents a curated digital exhibition of historical documentary photographs focused on labor, migration, poverty, and structural inequality. The project is grounded in sociological research and uses archival images to examine how economic systems shaped everyday life in the past and continue to matter in the present.

Artist Statement

This project is a curated collection of historical documentary photographs that examine inequality through a sociological lens.

My work draws on historical images to examine how inequality became ingrained in everyday life. Through visual archives of child labor, immigrant and working-class communities, and domestic life, I examine how economic systems shape people’s circumstances and experiences. I do not view these as mere history. I view them as evidence of social structures that still shape inequality today.

Most of all I am interested in how industrialization both created opportunities and also took advantage of people. Photographs of child workers in factories, fields, and on street corners show that work was often necessary for survival. Photographs of immigrant housing and enclave communities show how economic growth depended on the underprivileged while offering them only minimal security in return.

By creating this collection I hope to inspire viewers to look directly at the human toll that has often come with “progress.” My process is focused on selecting documentary photographs that represent overlooked lives rather than heroic stories. I am presenting these photographs as a curated digital exhibition rather than as original artworks, using them to highlight how labor, migration, and poverty were visually documented. I want this to make viewers look beyond nostalgia and think about how people continue working, migrating, and struggling with poverty today.

Artist Bio

Mariano Burgos is a sociology student at the University of Hartford in West Hartford, Connecticut, currently focused on historical documentary photographs of labor, inequality, and migration. His practice is research-based and curatorial, using archival images to examine how economic systems and social structures are represented visually.

Burgos is especially interested in institutional inequality, social services, and vulnerable populations. His academic background in sociology and his experience in community-based nonprofit settings shape his approach to this work. Through curated photographic projects, he examines how labor, poverty, and migration connect historical conditions to contemporary social life.
