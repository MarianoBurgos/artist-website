Artist Statement

My work draws on historical images to examine how inequality became ingrained in everyday life. Through visual archives of child labor, immigrant and working-class communities, and domestic life, I examine how economic systems shape people’s circumstances and experiences. I do not view these as mere history. I view them as evidence of social structures that still shape inequality today.

Most of all I am interested in how industrialization both created opportunities and also took advantage of people. Photographs of child workers in factories, fields, and on street corners show that work was often necessary for survival. Photographs of immigrant housing and enclave communities show how economic growth depended on the underprivileged while offering them only minimal security in return.

By creating this collection I hope to inspire viewers to look directly at the human toll that has often come with “progress.” My process is focused on selecting documentary photographs that represent overlooked lives rather than heroic stories. I want this to make viewers look beyond nostalgia and think about how people continue working, migrating, and struggling with poverty today.

Artist Bio

Mariano Burgos is a student at the University of Hartford with a concentration in sociology, currently studying historical documentary photographs of labor, inequality, and migration. He investigates how economic systems are represented through visual analysis.

Interest areas for Burgos include institutional inequality, social services, and vulnerable populations. He has worked in community-based settings through nonprofit agencies, most recently providing direct services to families. His visual projects are tied to his general sociological interest in how institutional and economic forces shape individual and community lives.
