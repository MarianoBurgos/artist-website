Selected Images

This portfolio presents a curated archive of documentary photographs that examine how labor, migration, poverty, and social inequality were visually documented in early industrial society. Click any image to enlarge it.

Group of Breaker Boys, South Pittston, Pennsylvania
1911
Photographer: Lewis Hine
Medium: Photograph
Dimensions: Original archival photograph

This image documents child labor within industrial workspaces by showing a group of boys connected to coal work in Pennsylvania. It highlights how economic systems relied on young workers and how inequality became embedded in everyday life.

Source: Library of Congress archive

Coal Breaker Boys, South Pittston, Pennsylvania
1911
Photographer: Lewis Hine
Medium: Photograph
Dimensions: Original archival photograph

This image documents boys engaged in dangerous industrial labor. It reflects how class inequality shaped childhood experiences and how young workers were placed in physically demanding conditions during industrial expansion.

Source: Library of Congress archive

Textile Mill Workers, North Carolina
c. 1908–1910
Photographer: Lewis Hine
Medium: Photograph
Dimensions: Original archival photograph

This photograph illustrates factory labor and the human presence within industrial production. It shows how labor systems organized daily life and reinforced class inequality, patterns that continue to influence economic structures today.

Source: Library of Congress archive

Young Cotton Mill Worker, North Carolina
1908
Photographer: Lewis Hine
Medium: Photograph
Dimensions: Original archival photograph

This image reflects the vulnerability of children working within industrial environments. It emphasizes how economic necessity forced young individuals into labor and shaped unequal access to education, safety, and opportunity.

Source: Library of Congress archive

Newsboys Selling Newspapers, St. Louis, Missouri
1910
Photographer: Lewis Hine
Medium: Photograph
Dimensions: Original archival photograph

This photograph documents labor in a public urban setting rather than in a factory. It reveals how economic hardship shaped childhood experiences and shows that child labor extended into city streets and everyday public life.

Immigrant Family at Ellis Island, New York

Photographer: George H. Clinedinst (Library of Congress)
Medium: Photograph
Dimensions: Original archival photograph

This image documents migration and family life within the context of economic inequality. It highlights how immigration was tied to labor systems, opportunity, and the unstable conditions faced by many working-class communities.

Source: Library of Congress archive

Photographer: Lewis Hine
Medium: Photograph

This image documents a group of child workers outside their workplace, emphasizing the collective nature of labor exploitation. It reflects how industrial systems normalized child labor and how inequality shaped entire communities.
